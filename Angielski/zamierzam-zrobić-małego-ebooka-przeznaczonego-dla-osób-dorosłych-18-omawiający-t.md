### Part 1: Definitions & Classifications

---

### 1. THE ACTIONS: HOW TO DESCRIBE THE ACT

- **To Swear:** The most common and versatile verb. It shares its root with "taking an oath," implying a solemn declaration used for emphasis.
- **To Curse:** Primarily used in the US. Historically linked to calling down evil or divine punishment upon someone.
- **To Cuss:** An American colloquialism derived from "curse." It is generally considered slightly less formal than "to swear."
- **To Use Profanity:** The formal/academic way to describe the act of using vulgar language.
- **To Utter an Expletive:** A technical, often legalistic or journalistic way to describe a sudden outburst of swearing.

---

### 2. THE NOUNS: WHAT WE CALL THEM

- **Swear word:** The standard British and American term for any vulgarity.
- **Curse word:** Common in North America.
- **Cuss word:** Informal, American English.
- **Four-letter word:** A common euphemism referring to the fact that many English swear words (e.g., *f*ck\*, *sh*t\*, *h*ll\*) consist of four letters.
- **Expletive:** A word or phrase used to fill out a sentence without adding much meaning, often used as a synonym for a swear word in professional contexts (e.g., "The transcript was full of expletives").

### 4. ADVANCED ADDITIONS (THE MISSING PIECES)

- **Invective / Vituperation:** High-level vocabulary for speech that is intended to insult, abuse, or denounce someone vehemently.
- **Pottymouth:** A common idiomatic noun for someone who swears excessively and habitually.
- **Blue Language:** An old-fashioned but still recognized idiom. To "talk blue" means to use a lot of swear words or ribald jokes.
